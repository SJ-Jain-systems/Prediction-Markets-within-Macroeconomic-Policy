# 4. Institutional Adoption Pathway

The FEDS paper stops at "Kalshi is accurate." It never asks the follow-on
question: how would the Fed or Treasury actually *use* this data in a decision
process? That is a question of institutional design, not of forecasting
accuracy, and answering it is a prerequisite for the concrete recommendation in
Section 6. This section lays out what formal incorporation would mean, as a
spectrum from lowest to highest institutional commitment, and asks where
Kalshi sits today.

## What the FEDS paper already signals, without saying so explicitly

Two facts show the pathway has already started, informally:

- **The paper itself is a Fed Board FEDS working paper**, co-authored by a
  Federal Reserve Board economist (Diercks, corresponding author, at the
  Division of Monetary Affairs; the paper thanks members of the Monetary and
  Financial Market Analysis section). Staff at the Board are, at minimum,
  looking at this data seriously enough to write it up in the Board's own
  series. That is a soft, staff-level foothold worth naming as evidence the
  pathway is not hypothetical — it has *begun*.
- **The authors propose a dashboard.** They "intend (subject to approval) to
  make the distributional daily-level data publicly available via an
  interactive website at EconFutures.com … to facilitate further research and
  enable policymakers to easily monitor shifts in investors' beliefs in
  real-time" (p.4). That is, in substance, a proposed monitoring dashboard.
  This section treats it as the baseline proposal to evaluate and extend, not
  something to reinvent.

## A spectrum of institutional commitment

Formal incorporation is not one thing. It runs from an internal analyst habit
to a standing public data product, and the manipulation and legitimacy risks
rise as you move up the ladder:

- **(a) Internal staff monitoring tool.** Analysts watch a Kalshi dashboard the
  way they already watch fed funds futures — informally, no public citation.
  Lowest bar; arguably already happening, given this FEDS paper exists.
- **(b) Public staff-research citation.** More FEDS papers and staff notes cite
  Kalshi-implied probabilities, without it becoming an official communications
  input. Medium-low bar; this paper is itself an instance.
- **(c) Communications-level citation.** A governor or the Chair references
  "prediction markets are pricing X% odds of …" in a speech or press
  conference, the way officials already sometimes reference fed funds futures.
  Medium-high bar — and the first rung that raises the optics question (should
  a central bank point at a market where the public is betting on its own
  decisions?), which Section 5 and Section 6 take up.
- **(d) Standing public data feed / dashboard.** An official or
  official-adjacent real-time page — the NY Fed markets-desk model, analogous
  to how the NY Fed publishes SOFR and repo reference rates. Highest bar;
  requires the safeguards from Section 6 before it would be defensible.
